# The Spotlight

**Someone is accusing you. All you have is word order.**

A 20-minute projection lesson on emphasis for Year 10 — cleft sentences, emphatic auxiliaries, and inversion, taught as rhetoric under pressure rather than as grammatical forms.

**Live:** [justinsteinmetz.github.io/TheSpotlight](https://justinsteinmetz.github.io/TheSpotlight/)

---

## The premise

Most grammar lessons teach structures and hope students find a use for them. This one reverses the order: it puts students in a predicament — an accusation — and offers grammar as the way out. The emphasis structures of English are presented as three weapons, each with a distinct rhetorical role:

| Weapon | Structure | Role |
|---|---|---|
| The spotlight sentence | Cleft (*It was Marek who...* / *It wasn't me who...*) | Redirect blame |
| The counterpunch | Emphatic auxiliary (*I **did** do my homework*) | Reject the accusation |
| The closing speech | Inversion (*Never have I...*) | The final flourish |

The operating concept throughout is the **spotlight**: emphasis doesn't change the facts — it changes what people notice. The narrative frame is the **courtroom**: accusation → defence → counterpunch → closing speech → trial → verdict. The spotlight explains *how* emphasis works; the courtroom explains *why anyone would care*.

## The arc (≈ 20 minutes)

1. **The accusation** (3 min) — Three denials of the same charge, one per structure. Students vote: *who do you believe?* The vote is deliberately open — the most dramatic denial may sound the least credible, and that discussion (emphasis as a dial, not a switch) is the richest moment in the lesson if it surfaces.
2. **The reveal** — *Same facts. Different weapon.*
3. **The defence — clefts** (5 min) — A flat report (*Liam sent the photo to the class group yesterday*) is cut open and the spotlight cycles across it: the person, the timing, the content. Then the defensive cleft (*It wasn't me who...*), then a 60-second pair task deflecting a direct accusation.
4. **The counterpunch — emphatic auxiliaries** (4 min) — The structure students will actually use by Friday. Mechanism, then rapid-fire pairs: A accuses, B counterpunches, swap. The drill targets prosody — the stress on the auxiliary matters more than the rule.
5. **The closing speech — inversion** (3 min) — Receptive, not productive, by design. The prime-minister before/after, then the retrieval hook: the trailer voice. Students have heard *Never before has the world seen anything like it* in every epic trailer, even if they've never produced the structure. The production task is choral and theatrical — say it aloud in the deepest voice you own — which drills the word order and teaches the register simultaneously.
6. **The trial** (4 min) — The class believes you ruined the group project. The truth: Marek deleted the file, after you went home. Build a defence using one cleft and one counterpunch; closing speech for bonus. The model answer demonstrates *sequencing* — three weapons arranged into one argument, flourish saved for last.
7. **The verdict** (1 min) — *The spotlight doesn't change the facts. It changes what people notice.* Exit ticket: invent an accusation, defend yourself in one sentence.

## Why the structures are ordered this way

The sequence follows the production gradient for this level, not the textbook order. Clefts and emphatic auxiliaries are productive targets; inversion is a recognition target anchored to a register students already know intimately — the trailer voice. Giving inversion the most oxygen — the usual approach — teaches the structure students are least likely to ever use. Here it gets the least teaching time and the most memorable hook.

## Where this sits

This lesson is the smallest unit of a larger family. Emphasis is narrative construction at sentence scale: choosing which element of an event becomes the story. A student who can ask *where's the spotlight?* in a sentence is ready to ask *whose story is this?* in a headline, an advert, an apology statement, or a museum label. The grammar is the entry point; attention management is the lesson.

## Running it

- Single HTML file, no dependencies beyond Google Fonts (degrades to system serif offline).
- **→ / space / click** to advance — fragments reveal step by step within slides.
- **← / click left edge** (8% of screen) to go back.
- Timing cues for the teacher sit top-right; students see only the content.
- Type scales with the viewport (`clamp()`), built for washed-out projectors.
- The spotlight-cycle slide (slide 5) advances through three emphasis states of the same sentence before moving on — each click moves the spotlight.

## Teaching notes

- Read denial C aloud in full melodrama *before* the vote. If the class unanimously picks B without questioning C, the protest-too-much insight dies quietly — the performance does the work the slide can't.
- The trial scenario's timing detail (*after you went home*) is deliberate: it forces the time-cleft as well as the person-cleft.
- The closing line of the model defence is serious, not comic. An absurd inversion gets a laugh but tells students these tools are theatre — the opposite of the lesson's claim.
